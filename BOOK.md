# Ontology-Driven Project Management (ODPM)

## Building Shared Understanding Through Ontology

### A Journey with Champ

> Understanding is not a destination we arrive at. It is a journey we share.

---

## Preface

This book did not begin with a methodology.

It began with a conversation.

One question led to another.
One answer revealed another question.
Ideas evolved, were challenged, refined, and sometimes replaced.

This book is not the story of inventing a framework.

It is the story of discovering a better way to think together.

---

## Chapter 1: The Missing Piece

Most software projects already have excellent artifacts.

- PRDs
- User Stories
- Figma
- Architecture
- APIs
- Code
- Tests

Yet projects still fail.

Not because teams lack artifacts.

But because they lack shared understanding.

The quality of implementation rarely exceeds the quality of the team's shared understanding.

---

## Chapter 2: The World Before the Software

Software does not implement user stories.

Software implements a model of reality.

Before discussing implementation we should understand:

- What exists?
- How are things related?
- What rules govern this world?

This shared model is the ontology.

Ontology is not philosophy.

Ontology is simply the team's shared model of the world.

---

## Chapter 3: PRD Is Not the Problem

A PRD answers one question:

Why are we building this?

Example:

> As a user, I want to switch between vehicles.

This is sufficient.

It expresses intent.

It was never intended to describe the complete world.

The missing artifact is not a longer PRD.

It is the ontology.

---

## Chapter 4: The Three-Layer Model

```text
PRD (Intent)

    v

Ontology

    v

Everything else
```

Everything else includes:

- Design
- Architecture
- APIs
- Implementation
- Testing
- Documentation
- AI Agents
- Future technologies we haven't imagined yet

They all consume the same ontology.

---

## Chapter 5: Visualizing Ontology

Ontology should be visible enough to discuss.

But ODPM does not need a heavy modeling tool.

The practical starting point is a glossary plus a lightweight relationship diagram.

The glossary defines the concepts.

The diagram shows the most important relationships.

Together they give both precision and overview.

Different audiences may need different views of the same ontology.

The goal is not the perfect diagram.

The goal is Shared Understanding that can be reviewed, versioned, and improved.

---

## Chapter 6: Chase Understanding

Traditional projects gather requirements.

ODPM chases understanding.

Questions are not checklists.

Questions are tools for discovering reality.

Every answer creates another question.

Every question reveals another part of the world.

The first answer is rarely the final understanding.

---

## Chapter 7: Decision Readiness

The goal is not complete understanding.

Complete understanding is impossible.

Instead we seek Decision Readiness.

> Decision Readiness is the judgment that the team's current best understanding is sufficient to take the next responsible action.

Or, more simply:

> The remaining unanswered questions no longer prevent the next responsible decision.

---

## Chapter 8: Brownfield Projects

Most projects already exist.

The first responsibility of a newcomer is not reading documentation.

It is reconstructing the ontology.

Artifacts become evidence.

Architecture reveals responsibilities.

PRDs reveal intent.

Code reveals behavior.

People reveal experience.

Together they reveal the shared model of the world.

---

## Chapter 9: User Stories Revisited

User stories describe work.

Ontology describes the world.

A story should not redefine the world.

Multiple stories should reference the same ontology.

This reduces duplication and improves consistency.

---

## Chapter 10: The Boss Compass

These principles quietly guided the development of ODPM.

- Keep it simple and straightforward.
- Clarity over complexity.
- Principles over procedures.
- Questions over assumptions.
- Alignment over individual brilliance.
- Shared understanding before execution.

---

## Chapter 11: Conversations

Artifacts preserve understanding.

Questions discover understanding.

Shared understanding is not transferred.

It is co-created.

Good conversations are where ontology grows.

---

## Chapter 12: Snapshot Thinking

Every artifact is a snapshot.

Ontology evolves.

Architecture evolves.

Design evolves.

Our understanding evolves.

Rather than seeking perfection, we continuously refine our current best expression of reality.

Git versions code.

ODPM versions understanding.

---

## Epilogue

This book has no final chapter.

Because understanding never ends.

Every project,
every conversation,
every challenge,
every contradiction

becomes another opportunity to chase understanding together.

Perhaps that is what project management has always been.

Not managing projects.

But helping people discover reality together.
