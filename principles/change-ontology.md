# The Change Ontology

Change initiatives typically describe change as a list of activities.

ODPM describes change as the delta between two ontologies.

## Two Models

Every change has a current-state ontology and a target-state ontology.

**Current-state ontology**: what exists today, how things relate, what rules govern, what states matter.

**Target-state ontology**: what will exist after the change, how relationships will differ, what new rules will apply, what new states will matter.

## The Delta Is the Destination, Not the Plan

The ontology delta explains what must change.

But the change plan still needs sequencing, ownership, communication, timing, and risk management.

The delta is the destination. The plan is the route.

Tasks, communications, and training are how you close the gap.

The ontology is what you are closing the gap toward.

## Why This Matters

Most change failures are not execution failures.

They are shared understanding failures.

People implement the change they understand, not the change that was planned.

Building the target-state ontology — and sharing it — before execution begins is the most important change management work.

## How to Use This

1. Before designing the change, model the current state. What actually exists? What rules actually govern? (Not what the org chart says. What is real.)
2. Model the target state with the same four building blocks: Concepts, Relationships, Rules, States.
3. Make the delta between the two models visible and discussable.
4. Treat every point of confusion or resistance as a question about which model the person is holding.
