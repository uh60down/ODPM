# ODPM and PMBOK

PMBOK answers how to govern and deliver a project.

ODPM answers what the team needs to agree on before those processes produce anything useful.

They do not conflict. ODPM inserts one layer into PMBOK's structure — between the Project Charter and the Scope Statement.

> ODPM improves the quality of PMBOK's inputs, not PMBOK's processes.

---

## The Missing Layer

PMBOK has a Project Charter (why and high-level what), a Scope Statement (what to build), and a WBS (how to break it down).

None of these explicitly capture the shared model of the world behind the project.

ODPM makes that model explicit.

```text
Project Charter
(Intent + Requirements + Assumptions + Constraints)

    ↓

Ontology
(Shared Model of the World)

    ↓

Scope Statement → WBS → Schedule → Execution
```

---

## Across the Process Groups

**Initiating**

After the Project Charter is approved, extract the implicit ontology before Planning begins.

What concepts does the charter assume? What relationships? What rules and constraints? What states will deliverables move through?

Most projects skip this step. Each team member then builds a private model from the charter. Misalignment follows.

**Planning**

The Assumptions Log is ODPM's richest source of open questions.

Assumptions are unresolved ontology questions. Chase Understanding is what you do with them.

The Communications Plan becomes a One Ontology, Multiple Views exercise — different audiences need different entry points into the same shared model.

**Executing**

Stakeholder resistance can reveal an ontology gap.

When a stakeholder pushes back, the PMBOK response is "manage the stakeholder." The ODPM response is "what world are they seeing that we are not?"

That question is often faster and more durable than stakeholder management alone.

**Monitoring and Controlling**

Integrated Change Control is the most important PMBOK process for ODPM.

Every change request should be evaluated for whether it redefines the ontology — a concept, relationship, rule, state, or action.

A change that redefines a concept is architecturally significant.

A change that adds a task is not.

PMBOK's change control process does not make this distinction. ODPM does.

**Closing**

Lessons Learned is the natural ontology snapshot moment.

What did we learn about the world that we did not know at the start? What concepts were missing? What rules turned out to be wrong?

That is ontology refinement for the next project, not just process improvement.

## PMBOK Artifacts as ODPM Inputs

| PMBOK Artifact | ODPM Role |
|---|---|
| Project Charter | Contains intent, requirements, assumptions, and constraints from which an implicit ontology can be extracted |
| Assumptions Log | Unresolved ontology questions — Chase Understanding targets |
| Scope Statement | Work that executes the ontology |
| WBS | Delivery structure downstream of the ontology |
| Stakeholder Register | Stakeholders as Concepts in the project ontology |
| Risk Register | Significant risks may reveal concepts, states, or rules that should be reflected in the ontology |
| Change Request | Proposed ontology refinement |
| Communications Plan | One Ontology, Multiple Views — per audience |
| Lessons Learned | End-of-project ontology snapshot |

---

## Ontology Across Phase Gates

PMBOK is plan-driven. The ontology should be baselined in Planning and maintained through formal change control.

ODPM's Snapshot Thinking still applies: the baseline is the current best understanding, not the final truth.

```text
Initiating   → Initial ontology extracted from Project Charter
Planning     → Ontology baselined alongside Scope Statement
Executing    → Ontology maintained through change control
Closing      → Ontology archived as Lessons Learned
```

---

## PMBOK 6 vs PMBOK 7

PMBOK 6 is process-heavy. ODPM must be inserted explicitly — there is no natural home for shared understanding in its structure.

PMBOK 7 is principle-based. Several of its twelve principles are already ODPM-compatible: Stakeholders, Systems Thinking, Adaptability and Resilience, Complexity.

The fit with PMBOK 7 is more natural. The need is the same in both.

---

## What ODPM Does Not Change

PMBOK's knowledge areas, process groups, roles, and governance remain intact.

ODPM does not replace risk management, stakeholder management, scheduling, or change control.

It improves the shared understanding those processes depend on.

ODPM changes the quality of what enters those processes — a shared model of the world rather than each team member's private interpretation of the Project Charter.
